Bees

Bees are winged insects closely related to wasps and ants, known for their role in pollination and, in the case of the best-known bee species, the western honey bee, for producing honey. Bees are a monophyletic lineage within the superfamily Apoidea.

Overview

There are over 20,000 known species of bees in seven recognized biological families. Some species ‚Äì including honey bees, bumblebees, and stingless bees ‚Äì live socially in colonies while most species (>90%) ‚Äì including mason bees, carpenter bees, leafcutter bees, and sweat bees ‚Äì are solitary.

Bees are found on every continent except Antarctica, in every habitat on the planet that contains insect-pollinated flowering plants. The most common bees in the Northern Hemisphere are the Halictidae, or sweat bees, but they are small and often mistaken for wasps or flies.

Anatomy

Bees have a long, complex tongue (proboscis) that enables them to obtain the nectar from flowers. They have antennae almost universally made up of 13 segments in males and 12 in females. Bees all have two pairs of wings, the hind pair being the smaller of the two.

The smallest bee is Perdita minima, less than 2 mm (0.08 in) long. The largest bee in the world is Megachile pluto, which can grow to 39 mm (1.54 in). Common honey bees range from 10-15 mm in length.

Behavior and Ecology

Bees play an important role in pollinating flowering plants and are the major type of pollinator in many ecosystems. It is estimated that one-third of the human food supply depends on pollination by insects, birds, and bats, most of which is accomplished by bees.

Bees focus their energies on gathering nectar and pollen, although they are able to learn visual patterns. A bee can navigate through complex mazes and can learn to use simple tools and observe and recall sequences of movements.

Types of Bees

Honey Bees

Honey bees (Apis) are the most well-known species and are managed worldwide for honey production and crop pollination. A single colony can contain 20,000 to 80,000 bees.

Bumblebees

Bumblebees (Bombus) are large, fuzzy bees that are important pollinators. They are social insects that form colonies with a single queen.

Carpenter Bees

Carpenter bees (Xylocopa) are large bees that build nests by tunneling into wood. Unlike honey bees, most carpenter bees are solitary.

Mason Bees

Mason bees (Osmia) are named for their habit of using mud or other materials to construct their nests. They are excellent pollinators for fruit trees.

Importance to Ecosystems

Bees are vital to biodiversity and food security. They pollinate approximately 80% of all flowering plants, including more than 130 types of fruits and vegetables. The economic value of bee pollination is estimated at over $200 billion annually worldwide.

Beyond food crops, bees pollinate plants that provide habitat and food for other wildlife, making them a keystone species in many ecosystems.
